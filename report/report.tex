\section*{Abstract}
\noindent
Brain tumor segmentation in magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is an important task for supporting
clinical diagnosis, treatment planning, and tumor monitoring. In this study, a deep learning-based
brain tumor segmentation pipeline is developed and evaluated using the Figshare Brain Tumor Dataset,
which contains 3,064 T1-weighted contrast-enhanced MRI images and corresponding binary tumor masks
collected from 233 patients. The pipeline extracts image--mask pairs from \texttt{.mat} files,
resizes each image to $256 \times 256$ pixels, applies normalization and data augmentation, and
evaluates model performance through five-fold cross-validation using an image-level splitting
strategy. Three experimental configurations are compared: a U-Net model with a ResNet-34 encoder
trained using a combined Dice and Binary Cross-Entropy (DiceBCE) loss function, a U-Net model
with the same loss function but with Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE)
applied during preprocessing, and a U-Net++ model with an EfficientNet-B4 encoder trained with the
DiceBCE loss function. All models share the same optimizer, scheduler, and training protocol,
ensuring a controlled comparison across configurations. Performance is measured using the Dice
Similarity Coefficient and Intersection-over-Union. Preliminary results from the U-Net++
EfficientNet-B4 configuration on fold~1 indicate a test Dice of 0.8706, which exceeds the
five-fold reference benchmark of 0.8608 reported for this architecture, suggesting the pipeline
is correctly implemented. Full cross-validation results for all three configurations are pending.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

The brain is the central organ responsible for controlling and regulating essential bodily
functions, including cognition, motor control, and sensory processing. The abnormal and
uncontrolled proliferation of cells within brain tissue leads to the formation of brain tumors,
which can significantly impair neurological function and pose serious health risks
\cite{louis2021who}. Brain tumors are broadly classified into benign and malignant types based
on their growth behavior, invasiveness, and histopathological characteristics. Benign tumors
typically exhibit slow growth and well-defined boundaries, whereas malignant tumors demonstrate
rapid proliferation, infiltrative growth, and aggressive invasion of surrounding healthy tissue
\cite{who2021tumors}.

Among malignant brain tumors, gliomas represent one of the most common and deadliest categories,
originating from glial cells within the central nervous system. High-grade gliomas, particularly
glioblastoma, are characterized by rapid progression, extensive infiltration, and resistance to
treatment, leading to a poor prognosis. Despite advances in surgery, radiotherapy, and
chemotherapy, the median survival time for patients diagnosed with high-grade gliomas remains
approximately 12--24 months \cite{ostrom2020cbtrus,louis2021who}.

Medical imaging plays a critical role in the diagnosis, treatment planning, and follow-up of
brain tumors. Among available imaging modalities, Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) is the
primary tool used in clinical practice due to its superior soft-tissue contrast and absence of
ionizing radiation \cite{shukla2017advanced}. MRI enables the visualization of tumor
sub-regions, such as the enhancing core, necrotic tissue, and surrounding edema, which are
essential for accurate diagnosis and therapeutic decision-making.

However, precise delineation of tumor boundaries from MRI scans is typically performed manually
by medical experts, making the process time-consuming, labor-intensive, and subject to
inter-observer variability \cite{amin2025dual}. Automated brain tumor segmentation remains
computationally challenging due to several intrinsic factors, including irregular tumor shapes,
fuzzy growth patterns, image noise artifacts, partial volume effects, and severe class imbalance
between tumor pixels and healthy tissue \cite{tohka2014partial}.

To address these challenges, recent research has focused on developing deep learning systems for
automated tumor segmentation. Among these methods, encoder--decoder architectures have become
widely adopted in medical image segmentation because they can learn high-level visual features
while preserving spatial details through skip connections. U-Net-based models are especially
suitable for this task because they produce pixel-wise predictions and can be trained directly
using paired MRI images and ground-truth tumor masks.

This project develops and evaluates an independent segmentation pipeline using the Figshare Brain
Tumor Dataset. Three experimental configurations are compared to assess how preprocessing,
architecture, and loss function selection affect binary brain tumor segmentation performance:
a baseline U-Net model trained with DiceBCE loss, a CLAHE-enhanced U-Net model trained with
DiceBCE loss, and a U-Net++ model with an EfficientNet-B4 encoder trained with DiceBCE loss.

The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related_works} reviews
existing studies on brain tumor segmentation. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} describes the
dataset, preprocessing steps, and segmentation models. Section~\ref{sec:Experiments} presents
the experimental setup and five-fold cross-validation strategy.
Section~\ref{EvaluationMetrics} defines the evaluation metrics. Section~\ref{sec:results}
presents and discusses the experimental results, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes
the study.


\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_works}

Brain tumor segmentation has been extensively studied, with earlier methods relying on
traditional machine learning and clustering techniques while more recent approaches leverage
deep learning architectures. A comparison of representative methods, organized by algorithm,
dataset, and reported Dice performance, is presented in Table~\ref{tab:related_works_comparison}.

Zhang et al.\ \cite{zhang2019brain} employed a hybrid clustering approach based on K-means,
achieving a Dice score of approximately 85\% on a private MRI dataset. As deep learning gained
prominence, Saouli et al.\ \cite{saouli2018fully} employed an Incremental Deep Neural Network
on the BRATS dataset, improving performance to 88.6\%.

Significant advances were made by Kamnitsas et al.\ \cite{kamnitsas2017efficient} with the 3D
CNN (DeepMedic) architecture, which effectively captures volumetric context and yields a Dice
score of 90.1\%. Building on the U-Net architecture, Allah et al.\ \cite{allah2023edge} proposed
the Edge U-Net, achieving 91.3\%, while Ruba et al.\ \cite{ruba2023brain} introduced the
JGate-AttResU-Net, pushing accuracy to 92.1\%.

These results demonstrate that improvements in network architecture, preprocessing, and loss
function design can produce consistent gains in segmentation accuracy. Based on this direction,
the current study develops an independent U-Net-based segmentation pipeline that compares a
baseline DiceBCE configuration, a CLAHE-enhanced DiceBCE configuration, and a U-Net++ model
with an EfficientNet-B4 encoder on the Figshare Brain Tumor Dataset.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

This section describes the dataset, preprocessing steps, segmentation models, training strategy,
and evaluation protocol used in this study. The project develops an independent deep learning
pipeline for binary brain tumor segmentation using the Figshare Brain Tumor Dataset. The primary
objective is to compare three U-Net-based segmentation configurations under a controlled
five-fold cross-validation setup.

The proposed workflow begins by extracting MRI images and tumor masks from the original
\texttt{.mat} files. The extracted image--mask pairs are resized, normalized, optionally
contrast-enhanced, augmented during training, and used to train segmentation models. The three
evaluated configurations are DiceBCE (U-Net), CLAHE DiceBCE (U-Net), and U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4
DiceBCE.


\subsection{Dataset}
\label{sub:dataset}

This study uses the Figshare Brain Tumor Dataset \cite{cheng2017figshare}, a publicly available
MRI dataset prepared for brain tumor analysis. The dataset contains 3,064 T1-weighted
contrast-enhanced MRI images collected from 233 patients. The data are provided in
\texttt{.mat} file format, where each file includes the MRI image, the corresponding binary tumor
mask, tumor class label, and patient identifier.

The task is formulated as binary semantic segmentation: each pixel is classified as either tumor
(foreground) or background. Although the dataset contains three tumor categories---meningioma,
glioma, and pituitary tumor---tumor type classification is outside the scope of this study.

The original \texttt{.mat} files are converted into paired image--mask samples. Each MRI image
is converted from grayscale to three-channel format to match the input requirements of
ImageNet-pretrained encoder weights. All images and masks are resized to $256 \times 256$ pixels.
Pixel intensity values are normalized using ImageNet mean and standard deviation statistics, and
tumor masks are converted into binary format (foreground = 1, background = 0) before training.

Data augmentation is applied during training to improve model generalization and reduce overfitting.
The augmentation strategy includes geometric and intensity-based transformations such as horizontal
and vertical flipping, rotation, brightness and contrast adjustment, elastic distortion, grid
distortion, optical distortion, coarse dropout, and Gaussian noise addition. In the CLAHE-based
experiment, Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization is applied to the MRI images during
preprocessing to enhance local contrast before augmentation and normalization.

\subsection{Algorithms}
\label{sub:algorithms}

This study implements and compares three deep learning configurations for binary brain tumor
segmentation. All experiments are based on semantic segmentation, where each pixel is classified
as either tumor or background. The three experimental configurations are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DiceBCE:} A U-Net model with a ResNet-34 encoder pre-trained on ImageNet,
          trained using a combined Dice and Binary Cross-Entropy loss function with equal
          weighting between the two loss terms.

    \item \textbf{CLAHE DiceBCE:} A U-Net model with the same ResNet-34 encoder and DiceBCE
          loss function, but with Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE) applied
          during preprocessing to enhance local image contrast before training.

    \item \textbf{U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 DiceBCE:} A U-Net++ model with an EfficientNet-B4
          encoder pre-trained on ImageNet, trained with the DiceBCE loss function. The batch
          size is reduced from 8 to 6 to accommodate the larger model's GPU memory requirements.
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{U-Net Architecture}
\label{sub:unet_architecture}

U-Net is an encoder--decoder convolutional neural network architecture widely used for biomedical
image segmentation \cite{ronneberger2015unet}. The encoder path extracts hierarchical image
features by applying convolutional operations and progressive downsampling, while the decoder path
reconstructs the spatial resolution to produce a pixel-wise segmentation mask. Skip connections
between corresponding encoder and decoder levels preserve spatial details that may be lost during
downsampling.

In the DiceBCE and CLAHE DiceBCE configurations, the U-Net model uses a ResNet-34 encoder
pre-trained on ImageNet as the feature extraction backbone. The decoder produces a single-channel
output of the same spatial resolution as the input, and a sigmoid activation is applied at
inference time to produce a tumor probability map. The binary mask is obtained by thresholding
at 0.5.


\subsubsection{CLAHE-Based Preprocessing}
\label{sub:clahe_preprocessing}

Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE) is applied in the CLAHE DiceBCE
configuration to enhance local contrast in MRI images before training. MRI scans may contain
low-contrast regions where tumor boundaries are difficult to distinguish from surrounding tissue.
CLAHE improves local intensity differences by performing histogram equalization on small,
overlapping image tiles while limiting noise amplification through a user-defined contrast limit.

The purpose of this experiment is to evaluate whether local contrast enhancement can improve
segmentation performance compared with the baseline DiceBCE configuration. The model architecture,
loss function, and all other training parameters remain identical between the two configurations,
so that the effect of CLAHE preprocessing can be assessed in isolation.


\subsubsection{U-Net++ with EfficientNet-B4 Encoder}
\label{sub:unetpp_effb4}

U-Net++ is an extension of the original U-Net architecture that redesigns the skip connections
using nested and densely connected convolutional pathways \cite{zhou2019unetpp}. This structure
reduces the semantic gap between encoder and decoder feature maps by inserting intermediate
nodes along the skip paths, enabling the model to combine multi-scale features more effectively
than standard skip connections.

In the third configuration, U-Net++ is combined with an EfficientNet-B4 encoder pre-trained on
ImageNet. EfficientNet-B4 employs compound scaling to balance network depth, width, and
resolution, providing richer feature representations than a ResNet-34 encoder. This configuration
is evaluated to determine whether the stronger encoder and nested skip-connection structure of
U-Net++ improves tumor segmentation performance relative to the baseline U-Net configurations.
The batch size is reduced from 8 to 6 to accommodate the larger model within GPU memory.


\subsubsection{Dice-BCE Loss Function}
\label{sub:dice_bce_loss}

All three configurations are trained using a combined Dice and Binary Cross-Entropy loss
function with equal weights. Binary Cross-Entropy supports pixel-wise classification between
tumor and background, while Dice loss directly optimizes the spatial overlap between the
predicted mask and the ground-truth mask. This combination is particularly appropriate for
medical segmentation tasks because tumor pixels typically occupy a much smaller area than the
background, making purely pixel-wise loss functions susceptible to class imbalance.

The combined loss function is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{DiceBCE} = \mathcal{L}_{Dice} + \mathcal{L}_{BCE}
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{L}_{Dice}$ is the Dice loss and $\mathcal{L}_{BCE}$ is the Binary Cross-Entropy
loss. By combining both terms with equal weight, the model is encouraged to produce accurate
pixel-level classification while also maximizing the spatial overlap between the predicted and
ground-truth tumor regions.


\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:Experiments}

This section describes the experimental setup used to train and evaluate the three segmentation
configurations. All experiments are conducted on the Figshare Brain Tumor Dataset using the
same data preparation procedure, image resolution, loss function, and evaluation protocol to
ensure a fair and controlled comparison.

The original MRI images and tumor masks are extracted from the dataset's \texttt{.mat} files
and converted into paired image--mask samples. Each image and its corresponding mask are resized
to $256 \times 256$ pixels. Grayscale MRI images are converted to three-channel format to match
the input requirements of the ImageNet-pretrained encoders. Intensity values are normalized
using ImageNet statistics, and the tumor masks are binarized before training.

Instead of using a single fixed train-test split, five-fold cross-validation is applied using
an image-level KFold splitting strategy. The dataset is divided into five folds of approximately
equal size. During each run, four folds are used for training (with a 15\% validation subset
drawn from the training pool) and one fold is reserved for testing. This process is repeated
five times so that each fold serves once as the test set. The final performance is reported as
the mean and standard deviation of the metric across the five test folds. The image-level
splitting strategy follows the same protocol used in the publicly available reference benchmark
for this dataset, enabling direct comparison of results.


\subsection{Experimental Configurations}
\label{sub:experimental_configurations}

Three experimental configurations are evaluated in this study:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DiceBCE:} A U-Net model with a ResNet-34 encoder trained with the combined
          Dice-BCE loss function using equal loss weights. This configuration serves as the
          baseline for evaluating segmentation performance without additional preprocessing.

    \item \textbf{CLAHE DiceBCE:} A U-Net model with the same ResNet-34 encoder and Dice-BCE
          loss function, but with CLAHE applied to MRI images during preprocessing. This
          experiment tests whether local contrast enhancement improves segmentation accuracy.

    \item \textbf{U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 DiceBCE:} A U-Net++ model with an EfficientNet-B4
          encoder trained with the Dice-BCE loss function. This configuration evaluates the
          benefit of using a stronger encoder and a more expressive skip-connection structure
          for brain tumor segmentation.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Training Parameters}
\label{sub:training_parameters}

All models are trained from image--mask pairs using the training parameters summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:training_params}. The Adam optimizer is used with an initial learning rate of
$10^{-4}$. The learning rate is reduced during training using a ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler,
which decreases the learning rate by a factor of 0.1 when the validation loss does not improve
for five consecutive epochs, with a minimum learning rate of $10^{-7}$. Training is stopped
early if the validation Dice score does not improve for 15 consecutive epochs. The best
checkpoint across all epochs is selected based on the highest validation Dice score.

Data augmentation is applied during training to improve generalization and reduce overfitting.
The augmentation strategy includes horizontal and vertical flipping, random rotation, brightness
and contrast variation, elastic transformation, grid distortion, optical distortion, coarse
dropout, and Gaussian noise. A fixed random seed of 42 is used across all experiments to ensure
reproducibility.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{EvaluationMetrics}

The performance of the segmentation models is evaluated by comparing the predicted tumor masks
with the corresponding ground-truth masks. Since the task is binary tumor segmentation, each
pixel is classified as either tumor or background. The two primary evaluation metrics used in
this study are the Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) and the Intersection-over-Union (IoU).

The Dice Similarity Coefficient is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    DSC = \frac{2 |P \cap G|}{|P| + |G|}
\end{equation}

where $P$ is the predicted tumor mask and $G$ is the ground-truth tumor mask.
The term $|P \cap G|$ represents the overlapping region between the predicted and actual tumor
areas. A Dice score close to 1 indicates strong spatial overlap and accurate segmentation, while
a score close to 0 indicates poor agreement with the ground truth.

The Intersection-over-Union is defined as:

\begin{equation}
    IoU = \frac{|P \cap G|}{|P \cup G|}
\end{equation}

where $|P \cup G|$ is the union of the predicted and ground-truth masks. IoU provides a
stricter measure of segmentation quality than DSC because the union penalizes false positives
and false negatives more strongly.

Both metrics are computed using globally pooled pixel counts across all images in a test fold
(micro-averaged reduction). This pooling strategy avoids the instability of averaging per-image
scores when the ground-truth mask is very small, which can occur for slices that contain only a
few tumor pixels.

In addition to evaluation, the training process uses the combined Dice and Binary Cross-Entropy
loss function defined as:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{DiceBCE} = \mathcal{L}_{Dice} + \mathcal{L}_{BCE}
\end{equation}

This combined loss is suitable for brain tumor segmentation because tumor regions typically
occupy a small fraction of the total image area. Optimizing only pixel-wise accuracy would
be misleading in the presence of such class imbalance, whereas Dice-based optimization
directly targets segmentation overlap.

\subsection{Discussion}
\label{sub:discussion}

The only completed result currently available is fold~1 of the U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 DiceBCE
configuration, which achieved a test Dice of 0.8706 and a test IoU of 0.7708. This result
exceeds the five-fold mean of 0.8608 reported in the public reference benchmark for the same
architecture on the Figshare dataset, which is consistent with expected fold-level variance:
individual folds will differ from the five-fold mean depending on the relative difficulty of
each test partition.

The training curve for fold~1 of the U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 configuration shows a healthy
learning progression. The validation Dice improved steadily from 0.732 at epoch~0 to a peak
of approximately 0.863 before early stopping was triggered at epoch~61. The learning rate
decayed appropriately through the ReduceLROnPlateau schedule, dropping from $10^{-4}$ to
$10^{-5}$ at epoch~30, to $10^{-6}$ at epoch~51, and to $10^{-7}$ at epoch~57. This decay
pattern is consistent with the model converging and the optimizer making progressively finer
adjustments. These observations suggest that the training pipeline is correctly implemented.

Results for folds~2--5 of the U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 configuration and for all five folds of
the DiceBCE and CLAHE DiceBCE configurations are pending. Once all evaluations are complete,
the following comparisons will be of primary interest:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{DiceBCE vs.\ CLAHE DiceBCE}: Isolates the effect of CLAHE preprocessing on
          segmentation accuracy when all other factors (architecture, loss function, training
          protocol) are held constant.

    \item \textbf{DiceBCE vs.\ U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 DiceBCE}: Isolates the combined effect
          of using a stronger encoder (EfficientNet-B4) and a more expressive skip-connection
          structure (U-Net++) relative to the baseline U-Net with ResNet-34.

    \item \textbf{CLAHE DiceBCE vs.\ U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 DiceBCE}: Compares preprocessing-
          based enhancement against architecture-based enhancement.
\end{itemize}

The preliminary fold~1 result for the U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 configuration is encouraging and
supports the hypothesis that a stronger encoder and nested skip connections can improve
segmentation accuracy on the Figshare Brain Tumor Dataset. Full conclusions will be drawn once
all five folds and all three configurations have been evaluated.


\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This study presents a deep learning-based pipeline for binary brain tumor segmentation using the
Figshare Brain Tumor Dataset. The pipeline converts the original \texttt{.mat} files into
image--mask pairs, resizes all samples to $256 \times 256$ pixels, applies normalization and
data augmentation, and evaluates model performance through five-fold cross-validation using an
image-level splitting strategy. Three experimental configurations are compared: a baseline U-Net
with a ResNet-34 encoder trained using the DiceBCE loss (DiceBCE), the same U-Net with CLAHE
applied during preprocessing (CLAHE DiceBCE), and a U-Net++ model with an EfficientNet-B4
encoder also trained with the DiceBCE loss (U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 DiceBCE).

All three configurations share the same optimizer (Adam, $lr = 10^{-4}$), learning rate
schedule (ReduceLROnPlateau), early stopping criterion (patience of 15 epochs on validation
Dice), and random seed (42), providing a controlled basis for comparison. Evaluation is
currently in progress. Fold~1 results for the U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 DiceBCE configuration
show a test Dice of 0.8706 and a test IoU of 0.7708, which are consistent with and slightly
above the reference benchmark of 0.8608 for this architecture. The training curve for this
fold indicates healthy convergence behavior with appropriate learning rate decay, suggesting
the pipeline is correctly implemented.

The remaining four folds for the U-Net++ EfficientNet-B4 configuration and all five folds for
the DiceBCE and CLAHE DiceBCE configurations are currently running. Once all evaluations are
complete, the five-fold mean Dice and IoU for each configuration will be reported and used to
draw conclusions regarding the relative impact of preprocessing (CLAHE), encoder strength
(EfficientNet-B4 vs.\ ResNet-34), and skip-connection design (U-Net++ vs.\ U-Net) on
segmentation performance.

Future work may include extending the pipeline to multi-class tumor segmentation, testing on
multi-modal datasets such as BraTS, exploring additional preprocessing strategies, and
investigating more advanced architectures such as transformer-based segmentation models to
further improve tumor boundary accuracy and generalization to unseen patients.
